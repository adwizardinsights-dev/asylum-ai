# The Five Protected Grounds for Asylum

Asylum protection requires that persecution be "on account of" one or more of five protected grounds. This is general informational content only.

## 1. Race
Persecution targeting a person because of their racial identity or membership in a racial group.
- Examples: targeting of ethnic minorities, racially motivated violence

## 2. Religion
Persecution based on religious beliefs, practice, or identity.
- Examples: targeting of religious minorities, forced conversion, punishment for practicing or not practicing religion

## 3. Nationality
Persecution based on national origin or citizenship.
- Examples: targeting of ethnic or national groups distinct from the ruling group

## 4. Political Opinion
Persecution for holding, expressing, or being perceived to hold political views.
- Can include "imputed" political opinion — when a persecutor attributes a political opinion to the person even if they do not hold it
- Examples: targeting of dissidents, journalists, activists, or family members of political figures

## 5. Membership in a Particular Social Group (PSG)
This is the most complex ground. A PSG generally must be:
- **Particular**: defined with specificity, not circular
- **Socially distinct**: recognized by society as a group
- **Cognizable**: share an immutable or fundamental characteristic

Examples of groups that have been recognized in some cases (not guaranteed):
- Families (immediate family members of someone targeted)
- LGBTQ+ individuals in certain countries
- Women facing certain forms of gender-based violence
- Former gang members who have renounced gang activity (outcome varies significantly)
- Witnesses to crimes in certain contexts

## Notes on Nexus Requirement
The harm must be "on account of" the protected ground — there must be a connection. Mixed-motive cases (where the actor has both protected and non-protected reasons) can still qualify if the protected ground was at least "one central reason."

## Important Limitations
- Economic persecution or general crime generally does not qualify
- Gang violence claims face significant legal scrutiny in current case law
- PSG definitions shift with legal precedent and policy — outcomes vary
